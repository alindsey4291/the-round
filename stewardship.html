What Stewardship is

Not a title — a practice in itself

A C.O.R.E Steward doesn't lead by having the best answers. You lead by protecting the conditions where people can change. That is the whole job.


Stewardship is the leadership tier of The Round. It includes everything in Diamond Companion, plus an eight-module certification curriculum, access to the Steward Council, a Circle kit license, and the official right to facilitate Circles within The Round.


You do not need to be finished to lead. You need to be practicing.

Who this is for

If you have ever been the steady one

Coaches, therapists, pastors, social workers, educators, veterans, and community leaders. People with no formal title who have always been the person others turn to in hard moments.


If you have experience holding space for others, Stewardship is where that experience gets structure, standards, and a community of peers who take the work as seriously as you do.

The curriculum

Eight modules. One standard.

The certification curriculum is designed to build competency, not just knowledge. Every module ends with practice exercises and assessment prompts. You lead practice Circles before your supervised session and final certification.

0

Onboarding and readiness

Strengths, growth edges, facilitation risks, Circle logistics, safety protocols, and confidentiality standards.

1

C.O.R.E framework mastery

Define each pillar in your own words. Deliver a three-minute explanation a newcomer would understand. Teach inclusion, not doctrine.

2

Facilitation skills — holding the room

Reflect before advising. Redirect with warmth. Manage silence, tangents, long-winded shares, and the person who fixes everyone else's problems.

3

Circle script and ritual design

Build your Circle script. Read it aloud. Time it. Remove any phrase that feels like belief pressure. Ritual means repeatable — not religious.

4

Ethical leadership and boundaries

Avoid dependency dynamics. Transparency beats pressure. Know when to refer out. Have a crisis protocol before you need one.

5

Conflict, repair, and accountability

Name impact without creating villains. Standards protect inclusion. Repair requires behavior change — not just words.

6

Coaching tools — from shame to evidence

Turn verdict language into open sentences. Build identity through small, repeated action. Ask before advising. Curiosity first.

7

Inclusion, culture, and community practice

Inclusion is maintained, not declared. Open Faith is freedom with standards. Culture lives in micro-moments: tone, interruptions, who gets heard.
